A new dual-stream analysis framework is presented in this dissertation. In order to guarantee high-quality input, the system first assesses the user's "voice cleanliness" by identifying background noise and distortion using a non-intrusive assessment model. After that, it transcribes the speech and uses a Graph Neural Network (GNN) based technique to analyze the narrative text. Specifically, it tracks entity states to identify long-range incoherence and contradictions. This unified pipeline provides a comprehensive evaluation of spoken narratives by bridging the gap between signal processing and semantic consistency checking.AcknowledgementsI wish to acknowledge my exceptional supervisor, Dr. Gehad Ismail, who has been a publisher's representative and provided constructive guidance and support to me throughout this dissertation and project. Systems that can listen to a user's story and offer intelligent feedback are becoming more necessary as automated educational tools and digital assistants proliferate. Nevertheless, existing systems frequently handle these aspects separately, evaluating speech quality without taking the plot into account or analyzing text without taking the fidelity of the audio into account.The problem of multimodal narrative evaluation is addressed in this dissertation. This project contributes to Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 4: Quality Education by creating a system that determines whether a user's voice is clean, free from artifacts that impede intelligibility and whether their story is coherent, that is, free from logical contradictions by offering strong tools for language learning and communication skills training.Problem DefinitionThe absence of integrated systems for assessing spoken narratives is the issue this study attempts to solve. Text can be transcribed by standard Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) systems, but they are unable to determine whether the audio quality is adequate for subsequent tasks, which frequently results in "garbage-in, garbage-out" errors. Furthermore, long-range inconsistencies like characters changing locations instantly or objects vanishing without explanation are frequently missed by standard text analysis models once they have been transcribed. A pipeline that thoroughly examines the narrative logic using structured entity tracking after verifying the audio signal quality is required.MotivationThe shortcomings of the existing automated feedback systems serve as the driving force behind this work. Only acoustic models can determine whether a recording is noisy, but they cannot determine whether the narrative makes sense. On the other hand, text-based coherence models depend on flawless transcripts, which are uncommon in real-world settings with subpar audio. A way to close this gap is provided by deep learning. While Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) can model the state of a story (who is where, doing what) to mathematically verify coherence, advanced non-intrusive speech assessment models like NISQA or models trained on QualiSpeech can serve as a quality gatekeeper. A comprehensive evaluation system that is both acoustically aware and semantically intelligent is made possible by integrating these technologies.Work MethodologyThis project employs a sequential pipeline methodology.Audio Analysis: The user's voice input is first processed by a deep learning model trained on the QualiSpeech dataset to assess voice cleanliness (noisiness, distortion, continuity).Transcription: High-quality audio is converted to text using an ASR model.Coherence Checking: The text is analysed by a GNN-based module trained on the ROCStories dataset. This module constructs a dynamic knowledge graph of the story's entities to detect logical contradictions (coherence violations).Target AudienceResearchers in computational linguistics and speech processing are the main target audience. Additionally, content moderation platforms that need to confirm the logic and quality of user-generated audio content, as well as educational technology (EdTech) developers building automated reading tutors or public speaking coaches, will find this work extremely pertinent.Main ContributionsUnified Evaluation Pipeline: A novel framework combining signal-level quality assessment with semantic-level coherence analysis.Non-Intrusive Voice Validation: Implementation of a Voice Cleanliness module that filters input based on perceptual quality metrics derived from the QualiSpeech dataset.Entity-Centric Coherence Checking: Adaptation of entity-tracking algorithms (typically used for generation) to evaluate user input for consistency errors. Related Work (State-of-The-Art)This chapter provides an overview of what is currently known about entity consistency, story coherence, and speech quality evaluation. It examines the development of state-of-the-art Large Language Models (LLMs) from early entity-grid models, pointing out crucial gaps in the way computers preserve high perceptual audio quality and long-range coherence.BackgroundSyntactic and semantic transformations have traditionally been used to represent text coherence, the quality that distinguishes a well-structured narrative from a random string of phrases. The Entity Grid model, which operationalized Centering Theory by tracking grammatical roles like the subject and object of entities across neighboring sentences to measure local coherence, was first developed by Barzilay and Lapata in their early foundational work. Similarly, Lin et al. showed that certain discourse relation transitions are preferred in coherent texts, and they proposed a Discourse Role Matrix to capture these patterns.The emphasis changed to learning distributed representations of text with the introduction of deep learning. By allowing models to pre-train deep bidirectional representations, BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) transformed the field and greatly enhanced performance on downstream tasks like Natural Language Inference and Question Answering. Even though these models are excellent at local fluency, they frequently have trouble with the long-range consistency needed for storytelling—a problem that is still a major area of current research.Literature SurveyTo address the complexities of modern coherence modelling and voice synthesis, this literature review is guided by the following research questions (RQs):RQ1: How do current state-of-the-art models maintain entity consistency and grounding over long narrative contexts?RQ2: What are the prevailing methodologies for evaluating coherence, and are standard metrics sufficient for measuring narrative quality?RQ3: How can structured reasoning and style-guided planning mitigate hallucinations in generated stories?RQ4: How can non-intrusive assessment models ensure voice cleanliness in generated audio without ground-truth reference signals?Search MethodologyUsing Google Scholar and arXiv sources, pertinent material was gathered to address these topics, with an emphasis on papers published between 2021 and 2025. Neural Story Generation, Entity Coherence, Discourse Relations, Hallucination Reduction, Stylized Text Generation, and Non-Intrusive Speech Assessment were among the keywords. Papers that suggested innovative architectural changes or fresh evaluation paradigms were given priority.Findings on Narrative CoherenceRecent research has moved beyond simple next-token prediction to more structured approaches for maintaining narrative consistency.Entity Consistency and Memory Mechanisms: A major challenge in narrative generation is maintaining the identity and attributes of characters over time. Papalampidi et al. [4] highlights that standard pre-trained Language Models (LMs) often lose track of protagonists in longer narratives. To address this, they proposed D-MNEMELM, a model augmented with a dynamic entity memory that uses cross-attention blocks to track and update entity states end-to-end. This approach significantly reduced perplexity and uncertainty regarding entity mentions compared to vanilla LMs [4].Visual Grounding and Chain-of-Thought (CoT): In the multimodal domain, Oliveira and Matos [5] addressed "referential hallucinations," where models lose track of character identity across visual frames. They introduced the Story Reasoning dataset and a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) framework that generates structured scene analyses such as tables of characters and objects before generating the story. Their Qwen Storyteller model demonstrated a 12.3% reduction in hallucinations by explicitly grounding narrative elements to visual IDs [5].Style and Planning: Coherence is not just about facts, but also about consistent style. Kong et al. [6] proposed a style-guided planning model that first predicts the distribution of stylistic keywords before generating the text. They introduced metrics like Lexical Style Consistency (LSC) to ensure the generated story adheres to the intended narrative tone [6].Critical Evaluation of Coherence: Pishdad et al. [7] critically examined the standard order discrimination task (determining if a shuffled sentence order is original), arguing it is an insufficient proxy for true coherence. They proposed four new tasks, including Connective Substitution and Story Cloze, to better capture causal and temporal consistency [7].Voice Cleanliness and Speech Quality AssessmentNarrative coherence guarantees a story's logical flow, but user engagement also depends on the story's perceptual quality and voice cleanliness. Traditionally, intrusive metrics such as PESQ, which demand a clean reference signal, were used to assess speech quality [8], [9]. Non-intrusive assessment techniques are necessary since ground-truth reference is rarely available in generative tasks like storytelling.Using raw audio waveforms as input, early non-intrusive models like AutoMOS (2016) showed that deep recurrent neural networks could simulate human Mean Opinion Scores (MOS) [8]. The emphasis has recently switched to deep learning architectures that can capture intricate temporal connections. To forecast naturalness scores, MOSNet (2019) introduced the usage of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Bidirectional LSTMs (BLSTMs), achieving a strong correlation with human assessments at the system level [9]. UTMOS (2022) introduced an ensemble technique that greatly outperformed baselines in out-of-domain scenarios by merging refined self-supervised learning models with simpler regression models to improve generalization [10].Multidimensional and descriptive assessment is now the focus of state-of-the-art research. To forecast not only overall quality but also particular aspects like Noisiness, Coloration, Discontinuity, and Loudness, NISQA (2021) uses CNN-Self-Attention architecture [11]. A more detailed diagnosis of audio issues is made possible by this breakdown. The most recent example of using Natural Language Reasoning for quality evaluation was the QualiSpeech dataset (2025) [12]. Models trained on QualiSpeech can produce comprehensive text descriptions of distortions rather than just a single scalar score, bridging the gap between signal processing and human perception. Furthermore, the UWB-NTIS system for VoiceMOS 2024, which successfully predicted ITU-T P.835 metrics by fine-tuning wav2vec 2.0 on artificially damaged data, shows that transfer learning is still essential for situations with limited data. [13].Analysis of the Related WorkDespite significant advancements, a clear research gap remains. It details the three-phase pipeline intended to process user audio and confirm story coherence, as well as the fundamental ideas and the datasets used to train the analytic modules.Basics and BackgroundTo provide a clear theoretical framework, this section defines the essential computational concepts and terminologies used in the system design.3.1.1 Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR)The technique that translates spoken language into written text is called Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR). Transformer-based deep learning architectures trained on massive volumes of multilingual audio data are used by contemporary ASR systems like OpenAI's Whisper. In this project, ASR acts as a link between the text-based coherence analysis module and the user's audio input.3.1.2 Deep Learning in AssessmentNon-Intrusive Speech Assessment: This refers to evaluating the quality of an audio signal without access to a "clean" reference file. Models like NISQA and those trained on QualiSpeech use Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to predict human-perceived quality metrics directly from the raw waveform.Graph Neural Networks (GNNs): GNNs are deep learning models designed to process data represented as graphs. In narrative analysis, they are used to model the relationships between characters (nodes) and their interactions (edges), allowing the system to track changes in the "story state" over time.3.1.3 Pre-processingAudio Pre-processing: Raw user input is down sampled and normalized to ensure consistent volume levels before being fed into the assessment model.Text Pre-processing: The transcribed text undergoes entity extraction and coreference resolution to prepare the data for the coherence checker.3.1.4 Chain-of-Thought (CoT) AnalysisChain-of-Thought (CoT) is a reasoning technique in which the model produces intermediate steps before to a conclusion. In this project, CoT is used for analysis rather than generation, asking the model to "think through" the user's tale step-by-step to clearly identify logical breaks.Dataset DescriptionTwo separate datasets are used for training the separate "Voice Cleanliness" and "Coherence" models.3.2.1 QualiSpeech Dataset (for Voice Cleanliness)The QualiSpeech dataset is employed to train the audio assessment module. It is a large-scale, fine-grained dataset designed to evaluate low-level speech quality.Content: It contains over 10,000 speech samples from diverse sources (synthetic and real-world) with various noise conditions.Annotations: It provides numerical scores (1–5) for Noisiness, Distortion, and Discontinuity, along with detailed natural language descriptions of these artifacts.Role: This dataset teaches the system to recognize "unclean" voice input (e.g., background static, robotic artifacts) that might degrade ASR performance.FeatureDescriptionScale / TypeOverall QualityThe general perceptual quality of the audio.1 (Bad) – 5 (Excellent)NoiseThe intrusiveness of background interference.1 (Very Intrusive) – 5 (Not Noticeable)DistortionPresence of unnatural artifacts (e.g., robotic voice).1 (Very Distorted) – 5 (Not Distorted)DiscontinuitySmoothness of speech flow (breaks, stutters).1 (Very Disjointed) – 5 (Very Smooth)DescriptionDetailed text explaining specific quality issues.Natural Language Text			    Table 3.1: Key Features of QualiSpeech 3.2.2 ROCStories Dataset (Coherence)The ROCStories dataset is utilized to train the coherence analysis module.Content: A corpus of five-sentence stories capturing everyday causal and temporal events.Role: This dataset provides ground-truth examples of "coherent" narrative flows. Verification.3.3.1 Phase 1: Voice Cleanliness AssessmentThe input to the system is the user's raw audio recording.Mechanism: A non-intrusive assessment model (based on the NISQA architecture) analyses the waveform.Process: The model extracts deep features to predict a Voice Cleanliness Score based on the QualiSpeech criteria (Noise, Distortion).Decision Gate: If the score is below a predefined threshold, the system rejects the input and prompts the user to record again in a quieter environment. This ensures that the subsequent ASR phase receives high-quality data.3.3.2 Phase 2: Speech-to-Text TranscriptionOnce the audio quality is validated, it is passed to the transcription module.Mechanism: An Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) engine converts the speech into a text string.Process: The audio is tokenized and transcribed. Punctuation restoration is applied to ensure the text is segmented into distinct sentences, which is crucial for the sentence-by-sentence analysis in the next phase.3.3.3 Phase 3: Narrative Coherence AnalysisThe final phase analyses the transcribed text for logical consistency.Mechanism: A GNN-based Entity Tracker processes the text.Process:Entity Extraction: The system identifies key entities (characters, objects).State Tracking: A dynamic graph is updated sentence-by-sentence. If "John" is at "The Park" in Sentence 1, the graph records Location (John) = Park.Conflict Detection: If Sentence 2 says John opened the fridge, the system checks the graph. Since Fridge is typically not in Park, the system flags a Coherence Violation.Output: The system returns a Coherence Score and highlights the specific sentence where the user's story lost logical consistency. (Figure 3.1: Pipeline Diagram)References[1] R.
